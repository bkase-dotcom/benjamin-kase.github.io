Audit Trail

A financial traceability tool for LA homelessness spending — from secondary research into systemic accountability failures, to an interactive prototype that makes every dollar traceable from budget allocation to verified outcome. Course project for GIXD-502 at ArtCenter College of Design.

The Problem

Los Angeles has spent billions on homelessness services with virtually no financial oversight. The problem isn't a lack of solutions — it's a lack of accountability infrastructure. Through desk research across audits, policy reports, and comparative case studies, a clear pattern emerged: data existed at every stage of the spending lifecycle, but no tool connected these stages into a traceable chain.

The Reframe

Transparency in this domain doesn't mean publishing reports and budgets. It means making spending traceable, verifiable, and legible throughout its entire lifecycle. Information existed in LA — it was just functionally unusable.

Key Findings

LA Court-Ordered Audit

$2.4B with No Financial Oversight

A court-ordered audit revealed LA spent $2.4 billion on homelessness services — auditors couldn't trace where the money went. LAHSA had no unified data system and in some cases didn't know who it was paying or what for.

California State Audit

30+ Programs, Zero Outcome Tracking

Nine state agencies running 30+ programs with no outcome tracking, making it impossible to determine which programs worked and which didn't.

San Jose 2025 Audit

Missed Targets Without Consequence

San Jose's audit revealed identical failures — service providers missed performance targets without consequence, replicating the same systemic pattern.

Houston — Success Case

55% Reduction Over a Decade

Houston achieved a 55% reduction in homelessness through coordination, sustained funding, and trusted data systems. Accountability infrastructure was the common thread.

Finland — Success Case

Near-Elimination of Chronic Homelessness

Finland's Housing First model nearly eliminated chronic homelessness. Like Houston, the success was built on coordination and credible data — political will sustained by public trust.

The Intervention

Audit Trail is a lo-fi interactive prototype of a financial traceability tool designed for LAHSA analysts, program managers, and oversight bodies. It creates a structured data chain where every link is explicit and verification status is visible in real time.

Seven Functional Screens

Each screen serves a specific role in the traceability chain. The core design decision: Missing vs Verified status makes gaps visible immediately — not discoverable years later in a formal audit.

Dashboard

KPI overview: total budget, spent to date, unverified spend flagged in red. Program spend chart. Live activity feed.

Budgets

Drill-down by fiscal year and program. Allocated vs. spent vs. remaining. Contracts within each program.

Contracts

Full contract table with vendor, program, cap, spend, and status. Click-through to transaction detail.

Transactions

Complete spend log with Verified / Missing evidence badges. Filterable. CSV export.

Evidence

Document attachment — invoices, outcome metrics, photos, field notes linked to individual transactions.

Audit Log

Immutable trace of who changed what, when, and why. Full accountability chain for every data modification.

Infrastructure for Trust

Audit Trail is infrastructure for trust, not just compliance. The research showed that public trust in homelessness spending is a prerequisite for the political will to sustain and expand funding. Publishing a PDF report is not transparency if no one can follow the numbers through the system.

Current State

Data exists at each stage of the spending lifecycle, but no tool connects these stages. Accountability is reactive — discovered years later through formal audits. Gaps are invisible until someone goes looking.

Audit Trail Model

Every link in the chain is explicit. Verification status is visible in real time. Gaps surface immediately — not as audit findings, but as red badges in the daily workflow of analysts and managers.

For Three Audiences

LAHSA Analysts

Trace spending anomalies through the full lifecycle — from budget allocation to contract to expenditure — with linked source data at each node. Surface unverified transactions before they become audit findings.

Program Managers

Monitor contract performance and evidence submission in real time. Missing evidence surfaces immediately, not months later. Vendor accountability is built into the daily workflow.

The Public

Simplified trust layer — verified spend, verification rates, and plain-language summaries. Taxpayers, journalists, and advocates can independently assess whether money is being spent accountably.

Core Design Decision

The Missing vs Verified state is the fundamental design choice. Traditional systems assume evidence exists until proven otherwise. Audit Trail inverts this — every transaction starts as unverified, and the default view makes gaps visible. This shifts accountability from reactive (audits) to proactive (daily operations).
